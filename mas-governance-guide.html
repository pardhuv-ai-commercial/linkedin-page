01 — Why this matters now

The regulatory picture just changed

On 22 January 2026, Singapore's Minister for Digital Development announced the Model AI Governance Framework for Agentic AI at the World Economic Forum — the world's first governance framework specifically designed for agentic AI systems. Simultaneously, MAS published proposed Guidelines on AI Risk Management for Financial Institutions. Both landed within weeks of each other. Together they define what regulators expect from financial institutions deploying autonomous AI agents.

The compliance posture problem

Most financial institutions in APAC have agentic AI programs already running — chatbots with tool access, automated case management, model-driven alert triage. The MGF arrived after deployment, not before. The practical question is: how does what you have stack up against what the framework now expects?

2019 — 2020
Model AI Governance Framework (original)
IMDA's foundational framework for AI governance — principles-based, voluntary, focused on traditional ML systems. Introduced concepts of transparency, accountability, and human oversight that now underpin the agentic framework.
May 2024
MGF for Generative AI
Extended the framework to large language models and generative systems — covering hallucination, bias, IP, content provenance, and systemic risk across nine governance dimensions.
November 2025
MAS AI Risk Management Guidelines (proposed)
MAS published a consultation paper proposing principles-based guidelines covering nine risk domains for financial institutions — the sector-specific layer on top of IMDA's cross-sector framework. Public comment closed January 2026.
22 January 2026 — Current
Model AI Governance Framework for Agentic AI
The world's first governance framework specifically for agentic AI systems. Four dimensions, 22 specific guidance points, open for public feedback and case study submissions. Voluntary but increasingly functioning as a procurement and regulatory benchmark.
02 — The framework

The four MGF dimensions — unpacked for practitioners

The MGF organises its guidance across four dimensions. Each dimension contains specific expectations for how financial institutions should design, deploy, and monitor agentic AI. These are not abstract principles — they translate directly into architecture decisions.

01
Assess and bound the risks upfront
Before deployment · use-case selection · privilege scoping
Before deploying an agent, organisations must conduct rigorous risk assessment considering: domain tolerance for error, exposure to sensitive data, reversibility of actions, level of autonomy, and task complexity. Agents must have unique identities tied to supervising users or agents for accountability.
Limit agent access to minimum required tools and data — deny-by-default
Define standard operating procedures for agent decision-making
Design offline mechanisms for malfunctions — kill switch required
Conduct threat modelling: memory poisoning, tool misuse, privilege escalation
Assign unique agent identities — traceable to a human supervisor
02
Make humans meaningfully accountable
HITL design · automation bias · responsibility allocation
The MGF emphasises human accountability as paramount. Multiple actors across the agent lifecycle can diffuse accountability — this must be explicitly prevented. Organisations must define significant checkpoints requiring human approval and actively combat automation bias through training and audit.
Define clear responsibility allocation across all parties in the agent chain
Implement contractual accountability provisions for external vendors
Human approval gates at checkpoints defined by risk level — not just "HITL" as a checkbox
Regular audits of HITL effectiveness — are approvals substantive or rubber stamps?
Anti-automation-bias training for all staff who approve agent actions
03
Implement technical controls throughout lifecycle
Testing · monitoring · access control · change management
Technical controls must be implemented across the full agent lifecycle — development, deployment, and ongoing operation. Testing must adapt for agentic-specific risks beyond standard software QA. Agents should be prompted to reflect on and log their plans for evaluation during development.
Baseline testing before production — including agentic-specific test cases
Whitelisted tool access enforced at infrastructure level — not in agent code
Real-time monitoring of agent behaviour with escalation protocols
Change management: document, test, and audit every update to agent configuration
Incident response playbooks specific to agent failure modes
04
Enable end-user responsibility
Transparency · training · override capability
End users — whether compliance analysts, relationship managers, or customers — must understand what agents can and cannot do, and how to override them. The MGF flags this as a governance gap in most current deployments: organisations invest heavily in the agent stack and almost nothing in user education and control design.
Clear disclosure when users are interacting with an AI agent
Documented user override mechanism — humans can take over any agent task
Training on agent capabilities, limitations, and when to escalate
Plain-language explanations of agent decisions — accessible to non-technical staff
03 — MAS layer

What MAS expects — the nine risk domains

The MAS AI Risk Management Guidelines (proposed November 2025, closed for comment January 2026) sit above the MGF as the financial-sector-specific layer. They are principles-based and risk-proportionate — but MAS places accountability with boards and senior management, which makes these expectation-setting documents, not optional reading.

MAS risk domain What MAS expects Agentic AI implication Status
Model & performance risk Accuracy, robustness, appropriate model choice for the task Agent model selection must be documented — why this LLM for this agent role, at this autonomy level Expected
Data governance Data provenance, quality, lineage, PDPA compliance Every piece of data an agent can access must be classified and audited. Cross-boundary data transfers require PII scrubbing documented in the audit trail Expected
Fairness & bias Assessment and mitigation of discriminatory outcomes For AML/KYC agents: false positive disparity across customer segments must be monitored. Analyst feedback rate is a proxy metric — if certain segments produce fewer feedback signals, model bias may be accumulating Expected
Transparency & explainability Internal explanations of model decisions; customer-facing where appropriate Every agent action must be reconstructable — prompt, tool call, output, approval. "The model decided" is not an acceptable explanation to a MAS examiner Scrutinised
Human oversight & control Defined HITL thresholds; duties for human reviewers The autonomy level registry (Observe / Recommend / Act-with-approval / Act-autonomously) must be documented, board-approved, and enforced at infrastructure level — not just stated in policy Scrutinised
Third-party & vendor risk Contractual and operational safeguards for external models and services Every vendor whose AI component an agent calls — LLM API, typology library, screening service — must be assessed. MAS expects the same rigour applied to third-party AI as to third-party systems generally Scrutinised
Cybersecurity & model security Protections against data exfiltration, model inversion, poisoning, adversarial attacks Prompt injection via agent inputs is the primary new attack surface. Fail-closed prompt injection guards are not optional — they are a cybersecurity control, not a nice-to-have Expected
Monitoring & change management Continuous performance monitoring; documented change controls The Evaluator Agent pattern (72-hour post-fix metric monitoring) directly satisfies this. Without it, change management for AI model updates relies on manual observation Expected
Incident response & escalation Playbooks for model failure and customer harm Every known failure mode (see Deployment Gap publication 01) must have a documented response playbook. "We didn't know this could happen" is not a defence when the failure mode is well-documented in the industry Expected
The examiner's three questions

MAS examiners will ask: "How do you know what your AI agent did? Why did it do it? Should it have?" If you cannot answer all three with specific, documented evidence, you have a finding. Traditional audit trails record that a user logged in and made a change. Agentic audit trails must record the agent's reasoning chain, every tool call, the data it accessed, and the human approval decision — all linked by a single incident ID.

04 — Gap analysis

Where most financial institutions fall short

Based on observed deployment patterns across APAC financial institutions and the failure modes documented in production, these are the gaps between current practice and MGF/MAS expectations. Most organisations have addressed the first column partially — very few have addressed it completely.

Current state — what most FIs have
HITL gate exists but is a rubber-stamp — analysts approve 40+ requests/day without review
Audit trail scattered across Teams, email, ITSM — no single linked incident chain
Agent permissions defined in code — engineers can change them with a PR, no board visibility
Vendor AI components assessed at procurement — not continuously monitored post-deployment
Prompt injection guard absent or advisory — fails open, not closed
Explainability covers model outputs — agent reasoning chain (prompt + tool calls + result) not retained
User training on AI tools generic — no specific guidance on when to override agents
Autonomy levels described in policy — not enforced at infrastructure layer
MGF/MAS required state — what good looks like
HITL volume monitored as a KPI — if analyst sees >15 requests/day, workflow reviewed for over-automation
Shared incident ID links every platform action — single reconstructable timeline for examiners
Permissions enforced by OPA policy at infrastructure level — board-signed, PR-controlled, auditable
Third-party AI assessed on the same cycle as third-party systems — annual minimum, trigger-based on incidents
Prompt injection guard is fail-closed — any uncertain input routes to HITL, not through to the agent
Full LLM call log retained: prompt + response + tool calls — reasoning chain reconstructable post-incident
Role-specific training: compliance analyst, operations, customer-facing staff — each knows their override pathway
Autonomy level registry enforced by infrastructure — code change cannot grant new permissions without policy update
05 — Action checklist

What to do — in the right order

Prioritised by regulatory urgency and implementation effort. Start with items 1–5 before your next MAS examination cycle. Items 6–12 form the fuller programme for institutions moving from pilot to scaled agentic deployment.

  • 01 Document your current agent inventory — every agent or near-agent system in production. Name, function, autonomy level, tools it has access to, and whether it was covered in your last model risk review. This is what MAS will ask for first. Risk / CRO
  • 02 Classify each agent's autonomy level using the four-tier registry (Observe / Recommend / Act-with-approval / Act-autonomously). If you have agents operating autonomously without documented board approval, that is the highest-priority finding. AI Governance
  • 03 Audit your HITL gate quality — not just whether it exists. How many approvals per analyst per day? What percentage are reviewed vs. clicked through? This is automation bias in action and MAS will probe it. Compliance
  • 04 Establish a shared incident ID standard across all platforms any agent touches — ITSM, communication tools, audit SIEM. The ability to reconstruct a complete incident timeline from a single ID is now a regulatory expectation. Technology / CISO
  • 05 Implement prompt injection guard as fail-closed on every agent that takes external input. This includes agents that process AML alert narratives, customer communications, or any field editable by parties outside the institution. Security / Engineering
  • 06 Move agent permissions to infrastructure-level enforcement (OPA or equivalent) — away from agent code. Engineers should not be able to grant an agent new tool access without a policy file change that goes through change management. Engineering / Architecture
  • 07 Retain full LLM call logs — prompt, response, tool calls, intermediate reasoning. Not just the final output. This is the evidence base for explainability when an examiner asks why an agent cleared a specific transaction or suppressed an alert. Technology / Compliance
  • 08 Apply third-party AI vendor assessment to every component in your agent chain — LLM API, typology library, screening service, embedding model. MAS third-party risk management guidance applies to AI components. Procurement / Risk
  • 09 Monitor analyst feedback volume as a leading indicator of model drift and automation bias. A drop in feedback rate — even without a model performance alert — signals degradation in the human oversight loop. AI Operations
  • 10 Design role-specific user training for each staff group that interacts with or is affected by agents — compliance analysts, operations staff, customer-facing teams. Each group needs different override and escalation guidance. L&D / Compliance
  • 11 Submit feedback or a case study to IMDA on the MGF. The framework is explicitly a living document. Contributors are acknowledged. This is how Singapore's governance standards get shaped — and it positions your institution as a responsible innovator. Government Affairs / CTO
  • 12 Schedule a formal Agentic Architecture Review within 90 days — covering all items above. Board-level AI risk reporting on agents and their autonomy levels is a MAS expectation. This review provides the evidence base for that reporting. CRO / CTO / Board
06 — Practitioner positions

How I read this landscape

Positions formed at the intersection of enterprise AI sales, technical architecture, and live deployment across APAC — not legal advice, but practitioner perspective on what actually matters.

On the MGF's voluntary status
"Voluntary frameworks don't stay voluntary for long in Singapore — ask anyone who ignored the 2019 edition"
IMDA's frameworks are formally non-binding but increasingly function as benchmarks in procurement, contracting, and regulatory discussions. The 2019 Model Framework was voluntary. By 2022 it was a de-facto procurement requirement for government-linked contracts. The Agentic AI MGF will follow the same path — faster, given the political urgency around AI governance.
On the governance gap statistic
"Only 14% of Singapore organisations have mature agentic AI governance — that's not a warning, it's a competitive window"
72% plan to deploy agentic AI within two years. 14% have mature governance. The institutions that close this gap first don't just reduce regulatory risk — they become the reference standard that regulators and peers will benchmark against. This is a first-mover opportunity, not just a compliance obligation.
On HITL design
"The worst HITL implementation is the one that exists on paper but creates automation bias in practice"
91% of compliance decision-makers say they'd compromise explainability for greater automation (industry survey, 2025). That is the statistic that concerns me most. A HITL gate that generates 40 approval requests per analyst per day is not oversight — it is liability coverage. MAS is right to probe the quality, not just the existence, of human review.
On commercial implications
"Governance-ready agentic AI will command a premium — the market is about to bifurcate"
Financial institutions that demonstrate MGF/MAS alignment will be able to move faster — not slower. Regulators reward demonstrated responsibility with faster approval cycles, greater deployment latitude, and access to industry pilots like Project MindForge. The compliance cost is real. The regulatory dividend for doing it right is larger.
